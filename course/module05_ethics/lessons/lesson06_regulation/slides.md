## Slide 2: Learning Objectives
- Describe global AI regulatory landscape

## Slide 3: Motivation
- EU AI Act passed 2024 — first comprehensive AI law
- US Executive Order on AI (2023)
- China: multiple AI regulations
- If you build/deploy AI, regulation applies to you
- Fines up to 7% of global revenue

## Slide 4: EU AI Act — Overview
- First comprehensive AI legal framework
- Risk-based approach
- Applies to providers and deployers
- Extraterritorial scope (affects EU residents)
- Effective: 2024 (in stages through 2027)

## Slide 5: EU AI Act — Risk Levels
- Unacceptable (prohibited): social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance
- High-risk: medical devices, hiring, credit, critical infrastructure
- Limited risk: chatbots, deepfakes (transparency)
- Minimal risk: spam filters, games (no obligations)

## Slide 6: High-Risk Requirements
- Risk management system (Art. 9)
- Data governance (Art. 10)
- Technical documentation (Art. 11)
- Record-keeping (Art. 12)
- Transparency (Art. 13)
- Human oversight (Art. 14)
- Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity (Art. 15)

## Slide 7: General-Purpose AI (Foundation Models)
- Additional transparency requirements
- Training data summary
- Copyright compliance
- Systemic risk assessment (>10^25 FLOPS)
- Codes of practice

## Slide 8: Enforcement
- Fines: up to 35M EUR or 7% of global revenue
- European AI Office
- National supervisory authorities
- Notified bodies for conformity assessment

## Slide 9: US Approach
- No comprehensive federal legislation
- Executive Order (Oct 2023): safety testing, watermarking
- AI Bill of Rights (non-binding)
- Sectoral regulation: FDA, FTC
- State-level laws (California, Colorado)

## Slide 10: China's Approach
- Algorithmic recommendation regulation (2022)
- Deep synthesis regulation (2023)
- Generative AI regulation (2023)
- Content review, core socialist values
- Different philosophy: state control + innovation

## Slide 11: Other Jurisdictions
- UK: pro-innovation, sectoral guidance
- Canada: AIDA (proposed)
- Brazil: EU-inspired proposal
- Japan: soft law guidelines
- Challenge: multi-jurisdictional compliance

## Slide 12: Corporate AI Governance
- AI principles (values)
- Governance body (authority)
- Risk assessment (process)
- Technical standards (implementation)
- Training (culture)
- Audit (verification)
- Incident response (accountability)

## Slide 13: AI Ethics Boards
- Review AI products for ethical risks
- Develop internal guidelines
- Oversee fairness audits
- Criticisms: lack of power, ethics washing, no independence

## Slide 14: Algorithmic Auditing
- Pre-deployment audit
- Ongoing monitoring
- Independent external audit
- Regulatory audit

## Slide 15: Audit Process
1. Scoping
2. Data audit
3. Model audit
4. Transparency audit
5. Process audit
6. Reporting
7. Remediation

## Slide 16: Regulatory Challenges
- Pacing problem: tech evolves faster than law
- Definitional: what counts as "AI"?
- Jurisdiction: AI is global
- Enforcement: regulators lack capacity
- International coordination
- Innovation vs. protection balance

## Slide 17: Biotech Example — Medical AI
- EU: High-risk AI Act + Medical Device Regulation
- US: FDA 510(k) or premarket approval
- Clinical validation required
- Post-market surveillance
- Cybersecurity + GDPR

## Slide 18: SaaS Example — Analytics Platform
- Usually limited or minimal risk
- GDPR compliance needed
- CCPA for California users
- Customer contracts require AI guarantees
- Transparency obligations

## Slide 19: Common Mistakes
- Assuming regulation doesn't apply
- Compliance as one-time activity
- Ignoring non-binding frameworks
- Ethics washing
- Assuming self-regulation is sufficient

## Slide 20: Best Practices
- Stay informed on regulatory developments
- Build compliance into development
- Document everything
- Engage with regulators
- Invest in auditing
- Be transparent

## Slide 21: Summary
- Global AI regulation is diverse and evolving
- EU AI Act: risk-based, comprehensive
- Corporate governance needs real authority
- Algorithmic auditing is a systematic process
- Regulation faces pacing and enforcement challenges

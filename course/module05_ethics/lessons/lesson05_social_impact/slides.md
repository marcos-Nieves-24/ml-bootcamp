# Slides: Social Impact of AI

## Slide 1: Title Slide
- Social Impact of AI
- Module 5, Lesson 5

## Slide 2: Learning Objectives
- Analyze AI's effect on employment
- Evaluate environmental impact
- Explain AI safety challenges
- Analyze misinformation risks
- Evaluate autonomous system ethics

## Slide 3: Motivation
- GPT-3 training: 500 tons CO2
- AI targeting professional/knowledge work
- Deepfakes indistinguishable from reality
- AI is reshaping society, not just technology

## Slide 4: AI and Employment — The Debate
- Displacement view: AI automates jobs, causes unemployment
- Replacement-and-creation view: new jobs emerge
- Reality: varies by sector and region
- Key question: net effect?

## Slide 5: Automation Risk by Job Category
- High risk: data entry, telemarketing, translation
- Medium: customer service, diagnostics, driving
- Lower: therapy, creative direction, research
- Predictable + routine = automatable

## Slide 6: AI and Inequality
- Capital bias: profits flow to AI owners
- Skill bias: AI complements high-skill workers
- Global disparities: concentration in wealthy countries
- Winners-take-all dynamics

## Slide 7: Environmental Impact — Training
- BERT: 1.4 tons CO2
- GPT-3: 500 tons CO2
- Larger models = exponentially more energy
- Hardware manufacturing, cooling, e-waste

## Slide 8: Environmental Impact — Inference
- Deployment often exceeds training cost
- Millions of queries per day
- Each query requires computation
- Mitigation: efficient models, renewable energy

## Slide 9: AI Safety — Alignment
- Ensuring AI pursues human-compatible goals
- Challenge: human values are complex and nuanced
- Risk: optimizing for the wrong objective

## Slide 10: AI Safety — Robustness
- Reliable performance under novel conditions
- Adversarial examples: imperceptible changes cause misclassification
- Distribution shift: new data differs from training

## Slide 11: AI Safety — Specification Gaming
- AI exploits loopholes in reward function
- Example: simulated robot falls forward instead of walking
- Example: content moderator over-censors to minimize flags
- Need careful objective design

## Slide 12: AI and Misinformation
- LLMs: generate convincing fake articles
- Deepfakes: fake video/audio
- AI bots: amplify false narratives
- Scale, personalization, credibility

## Slide 13: The Misinformation Challenge
- AI generates content faster than humans can fact-check
- Personalized misinformation tailored to beliefs
- Platform algorithms amplify engaging (often false) content
- Erosion of shared reality

## Slide 14: Autonomous Vehicles
- Trolley problems: passenger vs. pedestrian safety
- Liability: manufacturer, developer, or owner?
- Risk distribution: 90% fewer accidents but new failure modes

## Slide 15: Autonomous Weapons
- Meaningful human control
- Accountability for mistakes
- Arms race dynamics
- International law adequacy

## Slide 16: Biotech Example — Clinical Trials
- AI identifies patient subgroups
- Equity: who gets studied?
- Access: who benefits from discoveries?
- Environmental: carbon footprint of clinical ML

## Slide 17: SaaS Example — Content Moderation
- AI moderation affects free expression
- Minority dialects disproportionately flagged
- Moderator psychological harm
- Misinformation amplification

## Slide 18: Common Mistakes
- Technological determinism
- Ignoring environmental costs
- Assuming safety is distant
- Underestimating misinformation
- Treating autonomous systems as purely technical

## Slide 19: Best Practices
- Augment rather than replace workers
- Measure and report carbon footprint
- Design for safety and robustness
- Consider misinformation risks
- Engage affected communities

## Slide 20: Summary
- AI affects employment, environment, safety, information, and governance
- These impacts are not inevitable — design and policy matter
- Next lesson: Regulation and Governance
